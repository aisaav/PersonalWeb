Background to Project

Molecular Dynamics

Molecular dynamics is a computer simulation method for studying the movement of atoms in a particular medium. The benefit of using a molecular dynamic simulation is that it tracks different aspects of the atoms and can run for specific, controlled amounts of time. This allows for an accurate representation of how the atoms interact under specified conditions. Our project used Large-scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator (LAMMPS) molecular dynamics program.

Bootstrapping and Motivation

Bootstrapping is a statistical method reliant on computational calculations. Bootstrapping is the process of taking a randomized sample and developing new samples to calculate measures of accuracy such as confidence intervals. Bootstrapping takes a random sample of a predetermined size, treats this sample as a representative of the population it is trying to test. Once the original sample is taken, random elements from the original sample are chosen with replacement until the new sample becomes the predetermined size. Bootstrapping then continues to do this for N amount of times until it can quantify or produce some accuracy label for an estimate it makes.

When measuring the error of a diffusion constant, multiple tests are required whether it be done in a simulation or in a laboratory. The time, cost and difficulty of performing multiple tests has prompted the search of an alternate method: bootstrapping. Since the trajectories of nearby atoms are correlated, sampling a group of atoms could be used to better describe all the atoms. Thus, using bootstrapping on different atom percentages could potentially solve the issue of running multiple simulations and produce desired results.

Bootstrapping produces the best results when the original sample is random and independent: this causes an issue with testing atoms since their movements are correlated. Therefore, bootstrapping on a varying percentage and varying simulation time could determine how reliable this sampling method is in this context.

My Contribution

Developed a program that automated running of 200 simulations for each predetermined duration in LAMMPS and documented how atoms behaved in the simulator. I then averaged these samples to determine the 'true' diffusion coefficient for each duration. Using this output, I developed a second program dedicated to bootstrapping different sample sizes (12.5%, 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100%) for the both durations. For each sample size, 200 bootstraps were computed producing a diffusion coefficient and a margin of error to create a confidence interval for each bootstrap run. The third program I developed graphed the results and determined if the 'true' diffusion coefficient was within the confidence intervals the bootstrapping gave.
